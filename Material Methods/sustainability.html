Systems of Fashion: Code, Fibre, and Consequence

This page looks at sustainability not as a slogan but as a system: the servers that recommend a dress to you, the trucks that move it, the fibres that touch your skin, and the routes those fibres take when you’re finished with them.

Fashion is estimated to be responsible for a significant share of global greenhouse gas emissions and mounting textile waste. Instead of a list of “dos and don’ts”, this page traces how digital tools accelerate trends, how materials carry hidden water and carbon histories, and how brands are experimenting with circular design.

chapter one

The Invisible Digital Cost of Clothing

When people talk about “sustainable fashion”, most of the images we see are analog: cotton fields, dye houses, cutting tables. But between you and almost any garment sits a dense digital layer — recommendation engines, image servers, content-delivery networks and analytics dashboards that decide what appears on your screen.

Every time you open a shopping app, a few things happen at once:

  • High-resolution product photos and videos are streamed from servers.
  • Algorithms update which items to show you next based on your behaviour.
  • Analytics tools log clicks, scroll depth, wishlists and returns.

All of that computation runs in data centres that draw electricity from power grids that are not always renewable. Luxury groups like Kering and LVMH now include this digital footprint in their climate strategies, setting science-based targets and shifting offices, boutiques and parts of their digital infrastructure to renewable energy. At the same time, the growth of e-commerce has increased packaging waste and single-use plastics — from polybags to the estimated tens of billions of plastic hangers used each year worldwide.

A garment now carries two stories: one woven from cotton or polyester, and one woven from code and infrastructure.

This chapter is a reminder that the “online” part of online shopping is not weightless. Any serious attempt to decarbonise fashion has to include choices about hosting, content design, image compression and how many times a product photo needs to follow us around the internet.

Digital vs. physical impact (simplified)

Layer Examples Why it matters
Digital Servers, cloud storage, recommendation models Electricity use, hardware manufacturing, data-hungry design
Physical Farms, mills, factories, warehouses, shipping Water use, chemical impact, labour conditions, logistics emissions

The rest of this page moves between these layers, asking how they reinforce each other — and where there is room to slow things down.

chapter two

Algorithmic Acceleration: Why Trends Shrink in Time

Fashion once moved in seasons. Designers showed collections twice a year; buyers placed orders; clothes filtered into stores. Now, trend cycles can be measured in weeks or even days. Platforms read what we click on, pause on and share, and use that information to push similar images to millions of people at once.

In simplified form, the loop looks like this:

  • A silhouette or colour spikes in engagement on social platforms.
  • Algorithms amplify it to more users who behave similarly.
  • Suppliers receive signals and rush out lookalike versions.
  • E-commerce sites highlight “trending” pieces, reinforcing demand.

Consultancy analyses suggest that if nothing changes, fashion’s emissions could rise sharply by 2030. At the same time, other research shows that most brands could reduce their greenhouse gas emissions by more than half for a cost of around one to two percent of revenue by focusing on energy-efficient production, cleaner materials and better planning.

Fast vs. slower fashion models

Model Typical pattern Risks
Ultra-fast trend cycle New drops weekly, algorithm-led design, limited transparency Overproduction, waste, worker pressure, trend fatigue
Deliberate / circular Smaller runs, repair and resale built-in, clearer supply chains Higher upfront costs, slower growth, needs patient storytelling

Some brands are testing the slower model: seasonless collections, investment in traceable fibres, or digital passports that show where materials came from. Others still chase the metrics of pure volume. The same data that drives overproduction could also be used to plan fewer, better-made items — but only if the goal shifts from “sell as much as possible” to “sell enough, for longer”.

chapter three

Too Many Trends: Human Costs of the Infinite Wardrobe

An accelerated trend cycle isn’t only an environmental issue; it reshapes how people feel in their own clothes. Interviews with Gen Z shoppers describe a constant sense of being “behind” — that by the time they save up for a look they like, the feed has already moved on.

Common themes include:

  • A feeling that style is a test you are always about to fail.
  • Pressure to buy pieces that photograph well even if they don’t last.
  • Confusion about what counts as “good taste” when everything cycles so fast.
  • Exhaustion from trying to track micro-trends across multiple platforms.

At the production end, workers experience a different form of tempo: shorter lead times, last-minute order changes, and expectations that factories will absorb the risks of overstock or cancellations. Sustainability here is not just about emissions and water, but the pace at which human beings are asked to design, sew, ship and perform.

One response to this burnout has been a renewed interest in personal uniform dressing, thrifting and repeating outfits on purpose. These aren’t only aesthetic decisions; they are small refusals of a system that treats identity as something that expires on a monthly basis.

chapter four

Your Wardrobe as a Watershed

If the digital system decides what we want, the material system is what it takes to make it real. Life-cycle assessments of clothing highlight a few especially intense stages: growing and processing fibres, dyeing and finishing fabrics, and energy use in mills and factories. These are also the stages where brands have the biggest opportunity to cut emissions.

Thinking of your wardrobe as a small watershed can be helpful. Each garment has drawn on:

  • Water for irrigation, processing and repeated home washing.
  • Land for fibre crops or grazing animals.
  • Fossil fuels for synthetics, transport and heating.
  • Human labour and technical knowledge at every step.

Different fibres, different stories

  • Cotton: Renewable but often water- and pesticide-intensive, especially in dry regions without careful irrigation.
  • Polyester / nylon: Durable and cheap, but derived from fossil fuels and associated with microplastic shedding.
  • Viscose / rayon: Made from wood pulp; historically linked to deforestation and toxic processing, though some producers now use certified forests and closed-loop chemical systems.
  • Lyocell (e.g. TENCEL): A newer cellulosic fibre typically produced with more carefully managed solvents.
  • Wool and leather: Long-lasting, but connected to land-use impacts and animal welfare.

Brands across price points are experimenting here: from luxury houses investing in regenerative agriculture and next-generation fibres, to smaller labels that work with deadstock fabrics, artisan weavers and natural dyes. The wardrobe becomes the place where all of these experiments either succeed or stall — depending on how long garments are actually kept and used.

chapter five

Circular Design and Digital Tools

Circular fashion asks a simple question with complicated answers: instead of treating clothes as linear “take-make-discard” objects, how do we keep materials in use for as long as possible?

Luxury and mainstream brands are experimenting with different approaches. A few examples:

  • Stella McCartney invests in material innovation, from regenerated nylon to mycelium-based leather alternatives, and has long refused fur and exotic skins.
  • Gucci and other Kering brands test collections made with recycled polyester, organic cotton and bio-based materials, supported by clearer impact reporting.
  • Burberry donates surplus fabrics to design schools and runs capsule lines using recycled fibres, pairing heritage patterns with new material flows.
  • Chanel has launched a recycling platform focused on turning its own production waste into high-quality materials for future collections, treating circularity as long-term infrastructure.

How tech supports circularity

  • Resale and rental platforms catalog millions of garments.
  • Digital product passports store fibre and care information.
  • Computer vision tools help sort textiles by colour and material in recycling facilities.
  • Data dashboards allow brands to see where unsold stock piles up — and design it out in future seasons.

None of these tools are perfect. Some rely on new kinds of labour; others are limited to higher-priced segments of the market. But they show that technology is not only capable of speeding up consumption. It can also be used to slow things down, connect garments to new owners, and make it easier to see where waste is being created in the first place.

chapter seven

Glossary: Where Fashion and Systems Meet

These terms appear in conversations about technology and sustainability in fashion. They describe parts of the system that are often hidden from everyday view but shape how clothes move through the world.

Dark warehouse
A highly automated warehouse that can operate with very few workers on site, sometimes literally with the lights off. In fashion, dark warehouses store and sort products for rapid delivery, guided by data from online orders and demand forecasts. They can increase efficiency, but also concentrate power and obscure working conditions.
SKU explosion
“SKU” stands for stock-keeping unit, a unique code for each product. When brands offer many variations of colour, cut, length and fit, the number of SKUs can grow dramatically. This “explosion” makes forecasting harder and can increase waste when items do not sell, because each micro-variation is produced in smaller, riskier amounts.
Data labelling for style
The process of tagging garments or images with labels such as “oversized blazer”, “mid-rise”, or “floral print.” These labels help algorithms recognise patterns in style and power search filters, recommendations and automated trend analysis. Behind every neat filter in an app lies a mix of human and machine labour deciding what counts as which style.
Digital twin (supply chain)
A digital twin is a virtual model of a real-world system. In fashion, a digital twin of a supply chain might show how materials move from farms to mills, factories, warehouses and stores, allowing companies to test changes virtually before making them. Used well, digital twins can make it easier to test circular or lower-impact scenarios before committing to real-world changes.
Embodied energy of computing
The total energy required to build, run and cool digital hardware. When we shop online, this embodied energy forms part of the impact, even though it is rarely counted in traditional fashion sustainability metrics. It includes the servers that host product images, the networks that move data and the devices in our hands.
Circular design loops
Design strategies that plan from the start for reuse, repair, resale and recycling. Instead of a straight line from production to landfill, circular loops aim to keep garments circulating through different forms of use for as long as possible. In a truly circular system, digital records and physical design work together so that materials can be recovered and re-used rather than lost.
